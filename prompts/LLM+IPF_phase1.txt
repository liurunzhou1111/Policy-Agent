## Task
Based on the variable semantics and statistical structure visible in the distributions above, identify the TOP 5 most important joint dependency relationships that should be modeled when generating a synthetic population. Rank them from most important (strongest real-world dependency) to least important.

Guidelines:
1. Each dependency subset can contain 2, 3, or even more variables that should be jointly modeled (e.g., ["gender", "age"], ["education", "household_income", "occupation"]).
2. A variable MAY appear in multiple dependency subsets if it participates in several important relationships (e.g., "gender" can appear in both ["gender", "age"] and ["gender", "occupation"]).
3. Use the available joint distributions as direct evidence of dependency: if the conditional distribution of one variable changes substantially across categories of another, they are dependent.
4. Variables that share known real-world relationships should be grouped together (e.g., gender with age, gender with occupation, education with income and occupation).
5. Rank the 5 subsets by importance: the strongest, most well-established demographic dependencies first.
6. Do NOT perform any computation — reason qualitatively about which variables are likely dependent.
7. For dependency subsets that DO NOT have a matching entry in the "Available Joint Distributions" above, you MUST provide an approximate joint distribution table in the "approximate_joints" field.
